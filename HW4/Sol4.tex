\section*{Question 1: Existence and Uniqueness of PDE Solutions}

Consider the one-dimensional Poisson problem
\[
-u''(x)=f(x), \qquad x\in(0,1), \qquad u(0)=u(1)=0.
\]

\subsection*{(a) Weak formulation in a Sobolev space}

Let \(v\in C_c^\infty(0,1)\). Multiplying the equation by \(v\) and integrating over
\((0,1)\) gives
\[
-\int_0^1 u''(x)v(x)\,dx=\int_0^1 f(x)v(x)\,dx.
\]
An integration by parts yields
\[
\int_0^1 u'(x)v'(x)\,dx=\int_0^1 f(x)v(x)\,dx,
\]
because \(v(0)=v(1)=0\). This identity suggests the weak problem
\[
\text{find } u\in V \text{ such that } a(u,v)=L(v)\quad \forall v\in V,
\]
with
\[
V=H_0^1(0,1), \qquad
a(u,v)=\int_0^1 u'(x)v'(x)\,dx, \qquad
L(v)=\int_0^1 f(x)v(x)\,dx.
\]

The natural solution space is therefore \(H_0^1(0,1)\), the closure of
\(C_c^\infty(0,1)\) in the \(H^1\) norm. The test functions are initially taken in
\(C_c^\infty(0,1)\); by density, the weak identity extends to every
\(v\in H_0^1(0,1)\).

On \(H_0^1(0,1)\) it is convenient to use the norm
\[
\|u\|_V:=\|u'\|_{L^2(0,1)}.
\]
By the Poincar\'e inequality this norm is equivalent to the usual \(H^1\) norm, so it
defines the same topology on \(V\). With this choice, the bilinear form is continuous:
\[
|a(u,v)|\le \|u'\|_{L^2}\|v'\|_{L^2}=\|u\|_V\|v\|_V.
\]
It is also coercive:
\[
a(u,u)=\|u'\|_{L^2}^2=\|u\|_V^2.
\]
Thus \(a\) is continuous and coercive on \(V\). If \(f\in L^2(0,1)\), then \(L\) is a
continuous linear functional on \(V\) since
\[
|L(v)|\le \|f\|_{L^2}\|v\|_{L^2}
\le C_P \|f\|_{L^2}\|v\|_V,
\]
where \(C_P\) is the Poincar\'e constant. More generally, one may assume
\(f\in H^{-1}(0,1)\).

\paragraph{Example.}
If \(f\equiv 1\), then the classical solution is
\[
u(x)=\frac{x(1-x)}{2}.
\]
Indeed, \(u\in H_0^1(0,1)\), \(-u''=1\), and for every \(v\in H_0^1(0,1)\),
\[
\int_0^1 u'(x)v'(x)\,dx=\int_0^1 v(x)\,dx,
\]
so \(u\) satisfies the weak formulation.

\subsection*{(b) Lax--Milgram theorem and application}

\paragraph{Lax--Milgram theorem.}
Let \(V\) be a Hilbert space, let \(a:V\times V\to\R\) be continuous and coercive, and
let \(L\in V^\ast\). Then there exists a unique \(u\in V\) such that
\[
a(u,v)=L(v)\qquad \forall v\in V.
\]
Moreover,
\[
\|u\|_V\le \frac{1}{\alpha}\|L\|_{V^\ast},
\]
where \(\alpha>0\) is the coercivity constant.

\paragraph{Proof idea.}
Let \(J:V\to V^\ast\) be the Riesz isomorphism and define
\(\widetilde A:=J^{-1}A:V\to V\), where \(A:V\to V^\ast\) is given by
\[
(Au)(v)=a(u,v).
\]
Continuity of \(a\) implies that \(\widetilde A\) is bounded. Coercivity gives
\[
\alpha \|u\|_V^2\le a(u,u)=(\widetilde A u,u)_V\le \|\widetilde A u\|_V\|u\|_V,
\]
hence \(\|\widetilde A u\|_V\ge \alpha \|u\|_V\). Therefore \(\widetilde A\) is
injective and its range is closed. To prove surjectivity, let \(w\in V\) satisfy
\(w\perp R(\widetilde A)\). Then
\[
0=(\widetilde A u,w)_V=a(u,w)\qquad \forall u\in V.
\]
Choosing \(u=w\) gives
\[
0=a(w,w)\ge \alpha \|w\|_V^2,
\]
so \(w=0\). Hence \(R(\widetilde A)\) is dense and closed, therefore
\(R(\widetilde A)=V\). Thus \(\widetilde A\) is bijective, which is equivalent to
surjectivity of \(A\). This proves existence, and injectivity gives uniqueness.

\paragraph{Application to the Poisson equation.}
For the present problem, \(V=H_0^1(0,1)\) is a Hilbert space,
\(a(u,v)=\int_0^1 u'v'\,dx\) is continuous and coercive, and
\(L(v)=\int_0^1 fv\,dx\) is continuous whenever \(f\in L^2(0,1)\). All assumptions of
the Lax--Milgram theorem are therefore satisfied. Consequently, there exists a unique
weak solution \(u\in H_0^1(0,1)\) such that
\[
\int_0^1 u'(x)v'(x)\,dx=\int_0^1 f(x)v(x)\,dx\qquad \forall v\in H_0^1(0,1).
\]

\section*{Question 2: Kernel Ridge Regression}

Let \(\Omega=[0,1]^d\). The KRR estimator is defined by
\[
\hat f_{n,\lambda}\in \arg\min_{f\in \Hs}
\left\{
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i-f(X_i))^2+\lambda \|f\|_{\Hs}^2
\right\},
\qquad \lambda>0.
\]

\subsection*{(a) Representer theorem and the finite-dimensional system}

Set
\[
V_n:=\Span\{k(\cdot,X_1),\dots,k(\cdot,X_n)\}\subset \Hs.
\]
Every \(f\in \Hs\) admits an orthogonal decomposition
\[
f=f_\parallel+f_\perp, \qquad f_\parallel\in V_n,\quad f_\perp\perp V_n.
\]
By the reproducing property,
\[
f_\perp(X_i)=\langle f_\perp,k(\cdot,X_i)\rangle_{\Hs}=0
\qquad \text{for } i=1,\dots,n.
\]
Hence the empirical loss depends only on \(f_\parallel\), whereas
\[
\|f\|_{\Hs}^2=\|f_\parallel\|_{\Hs}^2+\|f_\perp\|_{\Hs}^2.
\]
If \(f_\perp\neq 0\), the regularization term is strictly larger while the data-fit term is
unchanged. Therefore every minimizer satisfies \(f_\perp=0\), and thus
\[
\hat f_{n,\lambda}(x)=\sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i k(x,X_i)
\]
for some coefficient vector \(\alpha=(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_n)^\top\in\R^n\).

Let \(K\in \R^{n\times n}\) be the Gram matrix,
\[
K_{ij}=k(X_i,X_j),
\]
and let \(Y=(Y_1,\dots,Y_n)^\top\). Then
\[
\hat f_{n,\lambda}(X_j)=\sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i k(X_j,X_i)=(K\alpha)_j,
\]
and
\[
\|\hat f_{n,\lambda}\|_{\Hs}^2
=\sum_{i,j=1}^n \alpha_i\alpha_j k(X_i,X_j)
=\alpha^\top K\alpha.
\]
The objective becomes
\[
J(\alpha)=\frac{1}{n}\|Y-K\alpha\|_2^2+\lambda \alpha^\top K\alpha.
\]
Differentiating with respect to \(\alpha\) gives
\[
\nabla J(\alpha)=\frac{2}{n}K\bigl((K+n\lambda I)\alpha-Y\bigr).
\]
Thus a canonical choice of coefficients is obtained from the linear system
\[
(K+n\lambda I)\alpha=Y.
\]
If \(K\) is singular, different coefficient vectors may represent the same function, but
the fitted function \(\hat f_{n,\lambda}\) is unique.

\subsection*{(b) Mean-square convergence and the role of \(\lambda_n\)}

Let \(T_k:L^2(\mu)\to L^2(\mu)\) be the kernel integral operator,
\[
[T_k g](x)=\int_\Omega k(x,t)g(t)\,d\mu(t),
\]
and let \((\mu_j,\phi_j)_{j\ge 1}\) be its eigenpairs. A standard consistency result for
KRR assumes two ingredients.

First, the target function satisfies a source condition of the form
\[
f_0=T_k^r g
\qquad \text{for some } r>0 \text{ and } g\in L^2(\mu),
\]
which quantifies the smoothness of \(f_0\) relative to the kernel. Second, the spectrum
of \(T_k\) is controlled through the effective dimension
\[
\mathcal N(\lambda):=\Tr\!\bigl(T_k(T_k+\lambda I)^{-1}\bigr),
\]
for example by an estimate
\[
\mathcal N(\lambda)\le C\lambda^{-\beta}, \qquad 0<\beta\le 1.
\]

Under these assumptions, standard KRR theory gives the risk bound
\[
R_n(\hat f_{n,\lambda},f_0)
=\E\|\hat f_{n,\lambda}-f_0\|_{L^2(\mu)}^2
\le C_1 \lambda^{2r}+C_2\frac{\mathcal N(\lambda)}{n}
\le C_1 \lambda^{2r}+C_3 \frac{\lambda^{-\beta}}{n}.
\]
The first term is the approximation error introduced by regularization; it vanishes when
\(\lambda\to 0\). The second term is the stochastic error produced by the noise and the
random design; it decreases when \(\lambda\) is not too small.

Therefore, if one chooses a deterministic sequence \(\lambda_n\) such that
\[
\lambda_n\to 0
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
\frac{\mathcal N(\lambda_n)}{n}\to 0,
\]
then both terms converge to zero and
\[
R_n(\hat f_{n,\lambda_n},f_0)\to 0.
\]
Under the polynomial bound \(\mathcal N(\lambda)\le C\lambda^{-\beta}\), it is enough to
require
\[
\lambda_n\to 0,
\qquad
n\lambda_n^\beta\to \infty.
\]
One common balancing choice is
\[
\lambda_n\asymp n^{-1/(2r+\beta)},
\]
which makes the bias and variance terms of the same order.

\section*{Question 3: Minimax Convergence Rate for KRR}

\subsection*{(a) Meaning of the minimax risk}

Let \(\Theta\subseteq \Hs\) be a model class of admissible regression functions. The
minimax risk is
\[
R_n^\ast
=\inf_{\tilde f_n}\sup_{f_0\in \Theta}
\E_{f_0}\|\tilde f_n-f_0\|_{L^2(\mu)}^2.
\]

\paragraph{Parameter space.}
The set \(\Theta\) specifies which true functions are allowed. In statistical terms,
Nature chooses an unknown \(f_0\in \Theta\).

\paragraph{Loss function.}
The quantity being evaluated is the squared prediction error in \(L^2(\mu)\),
\[
\|\tilde f_n-f_0\|_{L^2(\mu)}^2,
\]
and the expectation is taken over the random sample and the observation noise.

\paragraph{Infimum over estimators.}
The statistician may choose any measurable estimator \(\tilde f_n\) based on the data.
Taking the infimum asks for the best possible procedure among all estimators.

\paragraph{Supremum over \(f_0\).}
The supremum measures worst-case performance over the whole model class. An estimator is
judged by the most difficult function in \(\Theta\), not by an easy or favorable case.

For this reason, \(R_n^\ast\) is a benchmark for the problem itself: no estimation method
can have uniformly smaller risk than the minimax order over the entire class \(\Theta\).

\subsection*{(b) Bias--variance decomposition for KRR}

For fixed design points \(X_1,\dots,X_n\), define the sampling operator
\[
S_n:\Hs\to\R^n, \qquad
S_n f=(f(X_1),\dots,f(X_n))^\top,
\]
and the empirical covariance operator
\[
T_n:=\frac{1}{n}S_n^\ast S_n.
\]
Then the KRR estimator can be written as
\[
\hat f_{n,\lambda}=(T_n+\lambda I)^{-1}\frac{1}{n}S_n^\ast Y.
\]
Since \(Y=S_n f_0+\varepsilon\), where
\(\varepsilon=(\varepsilon_1,\dots,\varepsilon_n)^\top\), we have
\[
\hat f_{n,\lambda}
=(T_n+\lambda I)^{-1}T_n f_0
+(T_n+\lambda I)^{-1}\frac{1}{n}S_n^\ast \varepsilon.
\]
Subtracting \(f_0\) yields
\[
\hat f_{n,\lambda}-f_0
=-\lambda (T_n+\lambda I)^{-1}f_0
+(T_n+\lambda I)^{-1}\frac{1}{n}S_n^\ast \varepsilon.
\]

Taking conditional expectation with respect to the noise gives
\[
\E_\varepsilon[\hat f_{n,\lambda}\mid X]
=(T_n+\lambda I)^{-1}T_n f_0,
\]
so the conditional mean-square error decomposes as
\begin{align*}
\E_\varepsilon\!\left[\|\hat f_{n,\lambda}-f_0\|_{L^2(\mu)}^2\mid X\right]
&=
\left\|\lambda (T_n+\lambda I)^{-1}f_0\right\|_{L^2(\mu)}^2 \\
&\quad +
\E_\varepsilon\!\left[
\left\|(T_n+\lambda I)^{-1}\frac{1}{n}S_n^\ast \varepsilon\right\|_{L^2(\mu)}^2
\middle| X
\right].
\end{align*}
The cross term vanishes because \(\E_\varepsilon[\varepsilon\mid X]=0\). Averaging over
the design produces the full risk
\[
R_n(\hat f_{n,\lambda},f_0)=\E_X[\mathrm{Bias}_n(\lambda,X)]
+\E_X[\mathrm{Var}_n(\lambda,X)].
\]

The random quantities are \(X_1,\dots,X_n\), \(\varepsilon_1,\dots,\varepsilon_n\),
the operators \(S_n,T_n\), and therefore the estimator \(\hat f_{n,\lambda}\). The
fixed quantities are the kernel \(k\), the target function \(f_0\), and the prescribed
regularization level \(\lambda=\lambda_n\).

The role of \(\lambda_n\) is to balance the two terms. A large \(\lambda_n\) strongly
stabilizes the inverse \((T_n+\lambda_n I)^{-1}\), which suppresses variance but enlarges
the bias. A small \(\lambda_n\) reduces bias but amplifies fluctuations caused by the
noise. In the standard regime of Question 2 one obtains a proxy bound
\[
R_n(\hat f_{n,\lambda},f_0)\lesssim \lambda^{2r}+n^{-1}\lambda^{-\beta},
\]
and balancing the two contributions gives
\[
\lambda_n\asymp n^{-1/(2r+\beta)}.
\]
This short computation explains why the regularization parameter determines the final
convergence rate.
